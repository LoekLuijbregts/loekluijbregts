Picos de Europa

Where the mountains meet the sea — and shepherds have walked the same paths for 2,000 years

The context

Why this matters here

A landscape lived in, not looked at

The Picos de Europa were named by sailors returning from the Americas — these peaks were the first sight of home, the edge of Europe rising from the Atlantic. But long before any sailor spotted them, the Vaqueiros de Alzada were already here: Asturian nomadic herders who drove their cattle up to summer pastures every May and back down every October, a practice called transhumance that has continued unbroken for over 2,000 years.

The mountain villages — Bulnes, Sotres, Tielve — were built with stone so thick the winters couldn't get in. Families made cheese in the caves (Cabrales, still produced the same way today), fished for salmon in the gorges, and hunted brown bears and chamois in the high passes. The Picos were never a wilderness to escape to. They were home.

Why the world only just arrived

Spain's very first National Park was declared right here in 1918, yet for most of the 20th century the Picos remained quietly unknown outside Asturias. There were no ski resorts, no motorway, no airport. The only way in was the way the Vaqueiros had always used: on foot.

That changed slowly through the 1990s, when the Ruta del Cares appeared on the first trekking maps and outdoor magazines started calling it "Spain's best-kept secret." Today the Cares gorge trail gets 300,000 walkers a year — still nothing compared to the Alps, but enough to make it the defining reason visitors come to this corner of Asturias. Most still don't realise Pravia is the perfect base: 45 minutes from the park entrance, and without a single souvenir shop in sight.

Hiking Routes

10 Routes Worth Every Step

01

Ruta del Cares

The 'Divine Gorge' — 12 km carved into vertical limestone with the river thundering 500 m below. Start early. The path narrows to a ledge above nothing.

12 km · 4–5 hrs · Medium
02

Naranjo de Bulnes (Picu Urriellu)

The iconic orange limestone monolith that defines the Picos skyline. The approach through Pandébano is spectacular even without attempting the technical summit.

8 km to base · 3 hrs · Medium–Hard
03

Lagos de Covadonga

Two glacial lakes at 1,000 m, brilliant turquoise in summer, wreathed in mist in autumn. The road closes to cars at peak season — take the shuttle bus and have the trail to yourself.

Loop 6 km · 2 hrs · Easy
04

Vega de Ario

A high plateau accessible from Lago Enol with sweeping views across the Central Massif. One of the least-crowded routes in the park and among the most dramatic.

18 km round trip · 6 hrs · Hard
05

Pandébano to Bulnes

The ancient mule track to one of Spain's last car-free villages, now also served by a funicular. Walk down, take the cable car back up, have lunch at the village bar.

3.5 km descent · 1.5 hrs · Medium
06

Ruta de los Beyos

Following the Río Sella through a dramatic limestone gorge between Asturias and León. Accessible by car through the gorge, or on foot along the old road.

10 km · 3 hrs · Easy
07

Collado Jermoso

A high mountain refuge route through the Western Massif with wild ibex grazing on the crags. Stay overnight in the mountain hut and wake before sunrise.

14 km · 5 hrs · Hard
08

Desfiladero de la Hermida

The Deva river gorge that connects the Picos to Cantabria — a winding canyon of towering walls and medieval hermitages carved into the rock.

Drive + 5 km walk · 2 hrs · Easy
09

Fuente Dé Cable Car + Aliva Plateau

Take the cable car from 70 m to 1,823 m in four minutes, then walk the high moorland plateau where wild horses and chamois share the grass.

Cable car + 8 km loop · 3 hrs · Easy–Medium
10

Ruta del Alba

Following the Río Alba from Tarna through beech forest and meadows — quieter than the Cares, equally beautiful, and almost always empty midweek.

9 km · 3.5 hrs · Medium
Personal picks

Fleur & Carlos recommend

Two people, two perspectives — their actual favourite, not a curated list.

Fleur
Her number one
Lagos de Covadonga

"I've watched the sun set over Lago Ercina three times now and every time I cry a little. The colours — the water turns completely silver, the grass gold. It's half an hour from the casa and it feels like the end of the world in the best way. Go on a Tuesday, skip the weekend crowds, bring wine."

Carlos
His number one
Ruta del Cares

"This was the first hike I did when I came to Asturias, before I'd ever met Fleur, before any of this. I didn't know the region at all. I remember turning a corner in the gorge and stopping completely — I couldn't believe a place like this existed and I hadn't known about it. It's the reason I stayed."
