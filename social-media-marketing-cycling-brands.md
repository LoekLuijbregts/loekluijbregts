## The Social Media Problem Most Cycling Brands Have

Most cycling brands understand that social media matters. Few have a clear answer to why their content is not working. The symptoms are consistent: follower counts that grow slowly or not at all, content that generates likes from existing followers but does not reach new audiences, video views that plateau at a few hundred regardless of production quality, and an increasing sense that the algorithm is working against them.

The algorithm is not the problem. The content is.

Cycling audiences on social media are among the most discerning in any consumer category. They follow creators, athletes, and brands that reflect their own identity and values as riders. They share content that they would be proud to share — content that makes them look like someone who knows good cycling when they see it. They ignore content that is obviously produced to sell something, that is polished in ways that feel disconnected from the actual experience of riding, or that tells them things they already know in ways they have already seen.

This is the environment in which social media marketing for cycling brands operates. Getting it right requires understanding what cycling audiences actually respond to — and being willing to produce content that meets that standard rather than the standard that feels comfortable in a marketing meeting.

## What the Data Shows: GRAVAA and Pilot Cycles

Two recent case studies provide direct evidence of what social media growth looks like when the content strategy is right.

**GRAVAA, 2024.** GRAVAA entered 2024 as a Dutch cycling technology startup with limited social media presence. By the end of the year, the following across all channels had tripled. This growth did not come from a follower acquisition campaign, from paid promotion, or from a posting frequency increase. It came from a content strategy built around genuine performance moments — the Gravel World Championships and Paris-Roubaix — that gave the cycling social media audience something worth following. Riders who encountered GRAVAA content in the context of events they were already watching, featuring technology they found genuinely interesting, followed the account because they wanted to see more. That is the only social media growth mechanism that produces followers who stay and engage.

**Pilot Cycles, early 2026.** The earned media sprint for Pilot Cycles generated 4,500 new cross-platform followers in the February to March 2026 period. These followers came from content produced during the Morocco gravel adventure and CyclingWorld Düsseldorf — authentic, experience-led content that reached cycling audiences through the organic reshares of DT Swiss, Schwalbe, Pinion, and Vittoria. Each reshare introduced Pilot Cycles to a new audience that was already predisposed to be interested in a custom titanium bicycle manufacturer — because they were following component brands whose products go into those bikes.

In both cases, the social media growth was a consequence of content strategy rather than a social media tactic in itself. This is the critical distinction that most cycling brand social media strategies miss.

## The iPhone-First Principle

The most counter-intuitive consistent finding in cycling brand social media is that production quality is not the primary driver of content performance. In many cases it is inversely correlated with it.

A professionally produced brand video — colour-graded, drone-assisted, with a music licence and a voiceover — will typically underperform a shaky iPhone clip of a rider genuinely surprised by a climb, a view, or a mechanical failure they did not expect. This is not because cycling audiences dislike quality. It is because they prioritise truth, and they can tell the difference between content produced to document a genuine experience and content produced to serve a brief.

The iPhone-first principle is not a budget constraint. It is a creative choice based on what actually performs. Content produced with an iPhone by someone who is genuinely riding, genuinely testing, and genuinely experiencing the product or destination carries a credibility signal that no production budget can replicate. The imperfections are the proof. The wind noise, the unsteady camera, the moment where the rider laughs at something that went wrong — these are the details that tell the audience: this is real.

For cycling brands, the practical implication is significant. The decision to invest heavily in video production infrastructure or to commission high-end content from production companies is frequently the wrong allocation of the marketing budget. The return on a self-filmed adventure in demanding conditions, distributed through the right channels at the right moment, will in most cases substantially exceed the return on produced content of equivalent cost.

## Creator Partnerships: How They Work in Cycling

Creator partnerships are the most misunderstood element of cycling brand social media strategy. The standard approach — identify a creator with a large following, pay them to feature your product, measure the impressions — consistently underperforms relative to what creator partnerships can deliver when structured correctly.

The problem with the standard approach is that it purchases reach without purchasing credibility. A cycling creator with 200,000 followers who posts a sponsored feature of a product they have used for two days is delivering a media buy dressed as a recommendation. Cycling audiences have sophisticated instincts for identifying this, and their response — low engagement, minimal conversion, and a credibility cost to the creator that reduces the value of future sponsored content — reflects it.

Creator partnerships that generate genuine return share several characteristics.

**The creator is a genuine user of the product or a genuine visitor to the destination.** A bikepacker who has used a brand's bags across multiple multi-day adventures for years is a credible advocate. A cyclist who received the product three weeks ago for a one-day shoot is not. The difference is visible to the cycling audience.

**The partnership is structured around a genuine experience rather than a content deliverable.** A creator who goes to a destination or tests a product in genuinely demanding conditions and produces content from inside that experience will generate more valuable content than a creator briefed to produce a set number of posts in a set format. The brief constrains the authenticity. The experience produces it.

**The distribution is built into the partnership.** A creator with an engaged audience in the target demographic for the product or destination provides distribution that the brand cannot replicate through its own channels. The value is not just the content — it is the audience relationship that the creator has built and that the content participates in.

**The timeline is realistic.** Creator partnerships that are structured around immediate conversion — buy now, use code CREATOR10 — are rarely the right model for cycling brands selling products that require considered purchase decisions. A partnership structured around genuine, sustained engagement with the product over a riding season, with content produced at multiple points, builds a credible narrative that a single post cannot.

## Platform Strategy for Cycling Brands

Different platforms serve different functions in a cycling brand social media strategy, and treating them as interchangeable is one of the most common errors in cycling social media planning.

**Instagram** is the primary discovery and community platform for cycling brands. The visual nature of the platform is well suited to cycling content — landscapes, equipment detail, rider portraiture, action — and the cycling community on Instagram is large, engaged, and internationally connected. Reels are currently the primary growth mechanism on Instagram, with short-form video content reaching non-followers through the Explore and Reels feeds in ways that static posts and Stories cannot.

The Instagram content that performs for cycling brands is specific and authentic. Routes ridden, products tested in real conditions, moments from events and adventures that make the viewer want to be there. Generic motivational content, stock-feeling product shots, and text-heavy informational posts consistently underperform.

**YouTube** is the long-form storytelling platform. Multi-day adventures, route documentaries, product deep-dives, and event coverage find audiences on YouTube that Instagram's format cannot accommodate. YouTube content has a substantially longer shelf life than Instagram — a well-produced adventure video continues to generate views for years. For cycling brands whose product story requires time and context to tell — custom bike manufacturing, complex technology, multi-destination cycling tourism — YouTube provides the format.

**Komoot** functions as a social platform as well as a route research tool. Following a brand or creator on Komoot means receiving their routes in a planning context — which is fundamentally different from following them on Instagram. A cycling brand that publishes routes on Komoot — routes that feature their products in genuinely relevant conditions — reaches users who are in an active planning mindset rather than a passive browsing one.

**LinkedIn** is underused by cycling brands and significantly underused by cycling tourism destinations. The decision-makers who commission cycling tourism marketing — DMO directors, tourism board heads, destination development managers — are on LinkedIn. A cycling brand or destination with a strong LinkedIn presence, producing content that demonstrates strategic and commercial competence alongside the riding content, reaches a B2B audience that Instagram does not.

## The Reshare Economy in Cycling Social Media

One of the most powerful and least planned-for mechanisms in cycling brand social media is the organic reshare from component and partner brands. When content featuring a specific component — a DT Swiss wheel, a Pinion gearbox, a Vittoria tyre — is produced at a quality level that reflects well on that component, the brand that makes the component has an incentive to reshare it to their own audience.

This mechanism generated significant amplification for the Pilot Cycles sprint in early 2026. Content produced at CyclingWorld Düsseldorf featuring Pilot Cycles titanium bikes — equipped with Pinion gearing, DT Swiss forks, and Vittoria tyres — was reshared organically by all three component brands. Each reshare reached an audience that was precisely matched to the target customer profile for a custom titanium bike: technically informed, quality-oriented, already interested in the components that go into premium builds.

This amplification cannot be guaranteed, but it can be engineered. Content that features component brands in a genuinely flattering and interesting context — that shows their products performing in demanding conditions, or presented in a way that their own audience will find compelling — creates a mutual value proposition that most component brands will act on. It costs nothing beyond producing content that is good enough to be worth sharing.

## Posting Consistency Versus Content Quality

The conventional social media advice — post consistently, post frequently, maintain a content calendar — is not wrong, but it is frequently misapplied in cycling brand contexts. Posting mediocre content consistently is worse than posting excellent content less frequently, because every mediocre post is a signal to the algorithm and to the audience about the quality of what the brand produces.

The optimal approach for most cycling brands is a content calendar that creates space for genuine experiences — adventures, events, product testing in real conditions — and that produces content from inside those experiences rather than filling gaps between them with manufactured content.

This means accepting that there will be periods of lower posting frequency, and that those periods are preferable to periods of high-frequency mediocre content. It also means being willing to produce content opportunistically — to respond to moments that were not in the calendar because they are genuinely interesting — rather than rigidly adhering to a schedule that was set before the interesting things happened.

## Measurement: Followers, Engagement, and What Actually Matters

Follower count is a lagging indicator of social media strategy quality, not a leading one. The 4,500 new Pilot Cycles followers generated in the early 2026 sprint are valuable not because of the number itself but because of who they are — cycling enthusiasts who found Pilot Cycles content through organic distribution channels and chose to follow because they were genuinely interested. These are not followers acquired through a growth hack or a follow-for-follow scheme. They are potential customers.

The metrics that matter in cycling brand social media are:

**Follower quality over quantity.** A following of 5,000 highly engaged cyclists who are the actual target customer for a premium product is more commercially valuable than 50,000 followers assembled through generic growth tactics.

**Save rate on content.** Saves — when a user saves a post to return to it — are the strongest signal of genuine interest available on Instagram. Cyclists who save a route post, a product review, or an adventure recap are expressing planning intent. Save rate is a better indicator of content quality than like rate.

**Profile visits from non-followers.** When content reaches non-followers through Reels, Explore, or reshares, and those non-followers visit the profile, the content has done its primary job — introduced the brand to someone new. The conversion of profile visitors to followers, and followers to enquiries, is the social media funnel for cycling brands.

**Direct message volume and quality.** In cycling brand social media, the direct message is frequently where commercial conversations begin. Riders who are genuinely interested in a custom bike, a destination, or a product category will often make first contact through a DM rather than a formal enquiry form. Tracking DM volume and content provides a signal of commercial intent that aggregate engagement metrics do not.

## Summary

Social media marketing for cycling brands works when it is built on authentic content produced from inside genuine experiences, distributed through the channels where committed cyclists are already paying attention, and amplified through the component and partner brand relationships that the cycling industry's interconnected ecosystem makes available.

GRAVAA: social following tripled in 2024, zero paid promotion. Pilot Cycles: 4,500 new followers in six weeks, from two self-initiated trips.
