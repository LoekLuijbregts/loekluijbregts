100M
The North Star

One hundred million
new cyclists.
That's the goal.

One billion people already own a bike. Most of them stopped riding it. That's not an infrastructure problem — it's a story problem. Every campaign, every destination, every brand we help isn't just a client project. It's another kilometre toward that number.

The Problem

The bike isn't
the bottleneck.
The story is.

Walk into almost any garage in Europe and you'll find one. A bike leaning against the wall, tyres a little soft, saddle dusty. The person who owns it knows they should ride more. They probably want to. But something between the intention and the act keeps failing.

It's not the infrastructure. Infrastructure helps — but it follows culture, it doesn't create it. The cities that lead in cycling didn't build their way there first. They told their way there. Cycling became visible, normal, desirable. The roads and lanes followed.

The brands and destinations I work with are sitting on the same kind of untapped story. A gravel route nobody has heard of. A bike that solves a problem nobody knows exists. A region whose cycling potential is known to insiders but invisible to the world. The gap isn't product. It's distribution. It's the story not being told — or not being told in a way that travels.

The Mechanism

Earned media is
how culture moves.

Paid media can buy visibility. Earned media builds belief. When someone shares content about a route, a product, or a destination — not because they were paid to, but because it moved them — that's a different kind of reach. It travels further, sticks longer, and compounds over time without a budget attached to it.

The 110 million earned impressions across this work didn't come from a media buy or a boosted post. They came from riding the route, shooting it from the inside, and telling the story as it happened — not as a polished retrospective. Authentic, because it was real. Not because "authentic" appeared in the brief.

iPhone-first production isn't a budget constraint. It's a philosophy. The image from a camera bag on a rainy pass in the Atlas Mountains converts better than the image shot in a studio for the same brief — because it was actually there. The audience always knows the difference.
